The Pharmacist's Advice

FEVER

15-minute English version
Opening

LET'S TALK ABOUT
FEVER

INTRODUCTION

When fever appears, the first instinct is often to panic and look for the fastest possible way to bring the temperature down. But fever is not a disease in itself.

INTRODUCTION

In most cases, fever is a sign that the body is reacting to something, very often an infection, and that the immune system has been activated.

INTRODUCTION

The key is not to ignore fever and not to overreact either. It needs to be measured properly, interpreted in context and managed with good sense.

Section 1

WHAT IS
FEVER?

WHAT IS FEVER?

Fever means that body temperature is higher than usual. In everyday practice, many adults are considered to have a fever when a thermometer reads 38 C or more, although normal temperature can vary.

WHAT IS FEVER?

This is why one isolated number does not tell the whole story. Temperature can change during the day and can be influenced by the environment, clothing, physical effort and the method used for measurement.

WHAT IS FEVER?

Fever is usually a regulated response. The internal thermostat in the brain is reset to a higher level, often because immune signals are telling the body to react.

WHAT IS FEVER?

That is why people may feel cold and shivery at the beginning of a fever even though their temperature is rising. The body is trying to produce and conserve more heat.

WHAT IS FEVER?

Fever alone does not always tell us the exact cause of the problem. It tells us that the body is responding to something and must be interpreted together with the other symptoms.

Section 2

WHY DOES
FEVER HAPPEN?

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

Most fevers are caused by infections. Viruses are very common, especially in seasonal illnesses involving the nose, throat and airways, but bacteria can also be responsible.

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

Fever can also happen with inflammatory conditions, some autoimmune diseases, certain medicines and sometimes after vaccinations. Much more rarely, persistent fever can point to more complex medical problems.

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

During illness, the body releases chemical messengers that act on the temperature-regulating center in the brain and push the set point upward.

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

Once that happens, the body behaves as if its normal temperature were suddenly too low. This explains chills, shaking, cold hands and the drive to warm up.

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

Later, when the set point starts coming down again, sweating becomes more common and the person often feels hot, drained and uncomfortable.

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

Fever can be part of the immune response. It may make conditions less favorable for some germs and can support certain defense mechanisms of the body.

WHAT CAUSES FEVER?

At the same time, fever can make people feel miserable. Headache, muscle aches, weakness, sweating and poor appetite are common, so comfort still matters.

Section 3

HOW TO MEASURE
FEVER CORRECTLY

HOW TO MEASURE FEVER

If you want to manage fever well, you first need to measure it properly. A warm forehead can be a warning sign, but it is not enough for an accurate assessment.

HOW TO MEASURE FEVER

A digital thermometer is usually the most practical option. The important part is following the device instructions carefully and using the same method consistently when you compare readings.

HOW TO MEASURE FEVER

The result may vary slightly depending on where the temperature is taken. That is why consistency matters more than obsessing over tiny changes from one method to another.

HOW TO MEASURE FEVER

Try not to measure immediately after a hot drink, a cold drink, a hot bath, exercise or coming in from an extreme outdoor temperature. Waiting a few minutes can improve accuracy.

HOW TO MEASURE FEVER

And remember: the number on the thermometer should always be interpreted together with the person's alertness, breathing, hydration and overall appearance.

Section 4

MEDICINES:
WHAT THEY DO AND DON'T DO

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

Medicines that lower fever, such as acetaminophen or ibuprofen when appropriate, do not treat the cause. They mainly reduce discomfort and help the person rest and drink.

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

This is a crucial point. The goal is not always to force temperature back to perfect normality. The goal is often to improve comfort and function safely.

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

If someone is miserable, in pain, unable to rest or refusing fluids, a fever-reducing medicine may be helpful if it is suitable for that person and used correctly.

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

One common mistake is taking medicines too often, taking the wrong dose or combining several products without noticing they contain the same active ingredient.

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

Aspirin should not be given to children with fever unless a doctor has specifically advised it. In children, dosing always needs to match age, weight and the product instructions.

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

Pregnant women, older adults, and people with kidney disease, liver disease, ulcers, heart problems or regular medicines should be especially careful and should ask for advice before self-medicating.

MEDICINES AND FEVER RELIEF

Before alternating fever medicines or giving more than one treatment, it is always worth asking a pharmacist or doctor. The real danger is very often incorrect use, not lack of medicines.

Section 5

WHAT TO DO
AT HOME

WHAT TO DO AT HOME

In many everyday cases, fever can be managed at home with observation, comfort measures and medicines when appropriate. Rest matters because it allows the body to focus its energy on recovery.

WHAT TO DO AT HOME

Hydration matters as well. Fever and sweating can increase fluid loss, and dehydration can make a person feel much worse. Encourage regular fluids, especially in children and older adults.

WHAT TO DO AT HOME

Simple warning signs of dehydration include dry mouth, dark or infrequent urine, dizziness, unusual sleepiness and poor fluid intake. In babies, fewer wet diapers and fewer tears are important clues.

WHAT TO DO AT HOME

Do not over-wrap someone with fever to try to sweat it out. Light clothing and a comfortably cool room are usually better. Ice baths, alcohol rubs and harsh cooling methods are not routine solutions.

WHAT TO DO AT HOME

Food can stay simple. There is no need to force large meals if appetite is low, but fluids and light, tolerated foods are useful. If vomiting or diarrhea are present, hydration becomes an even bigger priority.

Section 6

WHEN TO GET
MEDICAL HELP

WHEN TO GET MEDICAL HELP

A baby under 3 months old with a fever needs urgent medical assessment. This is one of the most important fever rules to remember.

WHEN TO GET MEDICAL HELP

Medical help is also needed if fever comes with trouble breathing, severe headache, stiff neck, confusion, seizures, a new unusual rash, repeated vomiting or clear signs of severe dehydration.

WHEN TO GET MEDICAL HELP

Extra caution is needed for older adults, people with major chronic diseases, those receiving chemotherapy, and anyone whose immune system is weakened.

WHEN TO GET MEDICAL HELP

Even without major warning signs, it is reasonable to contact a doctor if fever lasts more than two or three days, keeps coming back, or the person is not improving as expected.

WHEN TO GET MEDICAL HELP

And always remember that context matters. A moderate fever in a confused or dehydrated person may deserve more concern than a higher fever in someone who is still alert and drinking well.

Section 7

TEN SIMPLE RULES
FOR FEVER MEDICINES

SAFE USE OF MEDICINES
  • 1Follow the recommended dose, timing and method of use.
  • 2Do not combine medicines without checking for overlap.
  • 3Do not change prescribed treatment on your own.
  • 4Always check the expiry date before use.
SAFE USE OF MEDICINES
  • 5Remember that some opened products expire sooner than the pack suggests.
  • 6Ask a pharmacist or doctor if you are unsure.
  • 7Keep the original box and leaflet whenever possible.
  • 8Store medicines as directed, usually in a cool dry place.
SAFE USE OF MEDICINES
  • 9Keep all medicines out of the reach of children.
  • 10Dispose of unused or expired medicines appropriately.
Closing

IN SUMMARY

CONCLUSION

Fever should be observed, measured properly, interpreted in context and managed with balance. It should not automatically trigger panic, but it should not be brushed off either.

CONCLUSION

A pharmacist can help you choose the right thermometer, use medicines safely and recognize when home care is still reasonable and when it is time to contact a doctor.

CONCLUSION

So the final message is simple: do not let panic guide you, but do not improvise either. Measure carefully, hydrate well, use medicines properly and ask for help in the right situations.
